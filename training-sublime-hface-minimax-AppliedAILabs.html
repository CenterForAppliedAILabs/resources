1. AI Basics — What LLMs Are & How to Think About Them

This section is the foundation. You don't need a computer science degree — you need an accurate mental model so that you know when to trust the AI, when to verify it, and when not to use it at all.

What is an LLM?

LLM stands for Large Language Model. It's a type of artificial intelligence that has been trained on enormous amounts of text (books, articles, websites, code, scientific papers) so that it can predict the most likely next word in a sequence.

That's it. At their core, today's generative AI systems — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, and the rest — are extremely sophisticated next-word predictors. The "magic" comes from doing that prediction at massive scale, across billions of parameters, on a training corpus that includes much of the public internet.

A useful mental model: Think of an LLM as an extremely well-read colleague who has skimmed millions of documents and can talk fluently about almost anything — but who also occasionally invents plausible-sounding nonsense with complete confidence. You would always fact-check that colleague before quoting them to a customer.

How are LLMs built?

Modern LLMs are built in three rough stages:

  1. Pre-training — The model reads a huge amount of text and learns statistical patterns of language. This is where the model forms its general "knowledge." This stage costs millions of dollars and weeks of GPU time.
  2. Fine-tuning — The model is further trained on curated, high-quality examples (often conversations, instructions, or domain material) so it learns to follow instructions and respond helpfully.
  3. Alignment / RLHF — Human reviewers rank model outputs. The model is then trained to prefer the kinds of responses humans rated higher. This is where "tone" and "safety" come from.

Most public chatbots you use are an LLM wrapped in a chat interface, sometimes connected to extra tools: a web search tool, a code interpreter, image generation, or connectors to your email and calendar.

Why do LLMs "make stuff up"?

The technical reason

LLMs generate text by predicting the most statistically likely next token. They do not have a built-in database of facts. They do not "know" that something is true. They know which tokens tend to follow other tokens in their training data.

That means if there isn't strong signal in the training data, the model will confidently fill in the gaps with the most plausible pattern — not the most plausible fact. We call these invented-but-confident statements hallucinations.

The practical implications

Always verify any specific number, regulation, drawing spec, or material property before you put it in a customer-facing document. Treat AI output like a draft from a new intern — useful, often right, but not authoritative.

This is especially important in medical device contexts, where Sublime Laser operates. Standards, biocompatibility claims, and FDA language must be exact. AI is a starting point, not the source of truth.

AI is more than just a tool

A common framing is to call AI "just another tool," like a calculator or a CNC machine. That's accurate, but it leaves out the most useful shift:

  • A calculator only does math. You tell it exactly what to compute.
  • A CNC machine only does what its program says. The G-code is the contract.
  • An LLM can interpret intent. You describe what you want in natural language and it figures out the steps.

That makes AI more like a collaborator than a tool. You collaborate with it the way you'd collaborate with a person: you give it context, you ask good questions, you push back when it's wrong, and you always own the final decision.

Trust boundary: Generative AI is best used for drafting, summarizing, brainstorming, organizing, and explaining. Treat it as a colleague, not as an oracle, and confirm anything that touches part drawings, ISO/ASTM specifications, regulatory claims, or customer commitments.

2. AI Chat Bot Interfaces — What's Actually Happening

Every "AI chatbot" you use is doing roughly the same thing under the hood: sending your text to a large language model and showing you its reply. The differences you experience — speed, tone, depth, accuracy, integrations — come from which model, how it's wrapped, and what tools it has access to.

The anatomy of a chatbot interface

Most chat interfaces, regardless of vendor, share the same parts:

💬 Input box

Where you type. Some bots also accept voice, image, file, or video input (uploaded files, screenshots, photos of parts, PDFs of drawings).

🧠 The model

The LLM doing the work. Different vendors use different models with different training, different "personalities," and different strengths. This is the biggest driver of output quality.

🛠 Tools & data

Many bots can call external tools: web search, code execution, image generation, calendar access, file retrieval, even email or document databases. The wrapper decides what the model can reach.

📚 Memory / context

Within a single chat, the bot remembers what you said earlier. Across chats, most bots start blank (for privacy). Some — Premium tiers, assistants like Le Chat, or agentic products — remember across sessions.

🎛 Controls

Model selector, "thinking / reasoning" toggle, temperature, web-search on/off, custom instructions, custom GPTs/Projects, etc. These let you shape the response.

🪪 Persona & policy

Vendor-specific behavior: tone, refusals, citation style, formatting defaults. Two bots running the same underlying model can feel very different because of this layer.

Why some chatbots are different from others

If I ask ten chatbots "what is the kerf width of a femtosecond laser on nitinol?," I'll get ten slightly different answers, some specific, some hand-wavy, some wrong. Here's why:

DifferenceWhat it changes
Underlying modelThe "brain." Different models know different things, reason differently, and have different context windows (how much text they can hold in one conversation).
Training data & cutoffA model trained on data through 2024 doesn't know events of 2025. Some bots get fresh data via live web search; others don't.
Tuning / personalityOne model may be cautious and precise; another may be friendly and verbose — even running the same base model.
Tools & integrationsA bot with web search can answer current questions; one without can only use what it was trained on. A bot with image generation can produce visuals; one without can't.
Domain specializationSome bots are tuned for coding, some for research, some for creative writing, some for enterprise document Q&A.
DeploymentPublic cloud vs. self-hosted vs. on-device (smaller models, like on your phone or laptop) changes speed, cost, and what data leaves your machine.
Practical takeaway: Pick the bot for the job. ChatGPT is the all-rounder. Claude tends to be the best writer and careful reader. Gemini is best if you live in Google Workspace. Copilot is best if you live in Microsoft 365. Perplexity is best for cited research. Open-source / local bots matter when data privacy is critical.

What "agentic" means

You'll see the word agentic more and more. A chatbot answers questions. An agent takes actions: it can read a file, search a database, send an email, run code, call an API, and chain multiple steps together without asking you at every turn. The newest wave of products — Gemini Spark, OpenClaw, Claude's computer-use features, ChatGPT's Agent mode — are all variations on this idea.

4. Prompting Skills — Strategies That Consistently Work

The biggest performance jump you'll get from AI isn't choosing a model — it's writing better prompts. The principles below work across every major chatbot.

The five universal rules

  1. Be specific about what you want. "Write me a follow-up email" is vague. "Write a 6-line follow-up email to a neurovascular R&D engineer who asked for a quote 10 days ago and went quiet" is a prompt.
  2. Give the model context. Tell it who you are, who the audience is, what the situation is, and what tone you want. The more relevant context, the less it has to guess.
  3. Specify the format. Ask for "a table with three columns," "five bullet points," or "a 4-line LinkedIn message." Output format drives output quality.
  4. Show, don't just tell. Include an example of what good looks like (few-shot prompting). It anchors the model's tone, length, and style.
  5. Iterate. Treat the first response as a draft. Tell the bot what to change: "Make it shorter," "More technical," "Less salesy," "Convert to a numbered list."

The Good / Better / Best framework

Every prompt in this course uses a Good / Better / Best structure so you can see what makes each tier better:

🟢 Good — the baseline

A plain, generic prompt. It usually works, but the output is generic too. Useful when speed matters more than nuance.

🟡 Better — with context

Adds who you are, who it's for, and the goal. Significantly improves relevance and tone.

🟣 Best — fully specified

Defines role, audience, constraints, format, length, and style. Includes an example or persona. Produces output you can often use with light editing.

Handy prompting patterns

🎭 Role prompt

"Act as a senior medical device manufacturing engineer with 20 years of laser machining experience. Now review this email I wrote..."

🪜 Chain-of-thought

"Think step by step before giving your final answer." Forces the model to reason before responding, improving accuracy on multi-step problems.

🧪 Few-shot

"Here are two example follow-up emails in our tone. Now write a third for this new situation." Gives the model a style anchor.

📋 Rubric

"Score your draft 1–5 on tone, clarity, and persuasion, then rewrite to improve any score under 4." Self-critique improves quality.

🪞 Format lock

"Respond only with valid JSON in this shape: {subject, body, cta}." Forces structured output you can paste into other tools.

🛑 Negative constraints

"Do not use technical jargon. Do not mention specific tolerances." Tells the model what to avoid, narrowing its range.

The most underrated rule: AI will confidently make things up. Add a sentence like "If you're not sure, say 'I'm not sure' rather than guessing" to almost any factual prompt — it cuts hallucinations dramatically.

1. Personalized cold outreach email

Good Baseline Outreach
Scenario: We just identified a new R&D lead at a neurovascular device company who might need precision laser-machined hypotubes.
Prompt
Write a cold outreach email to a medical device R&D engineer introducing Sublime Laser's femtosecond tube machining services.
Better With context Outreach
Same scenario, but now you give the model context, a target persona, and a goal.
Prompt
Write a cold outreach email to a senior R&D mechanical engineer at a mid-sized neurovascular device company. The engineer has 10+ years of experience and has likely sourced precision laser-machined nitinol and stainless-steel hypotubes in the past.

Goal: book a 20-minute intro call to discuss a possible new project. We are Sublime Laser — we specialize in femtosecond and fiber laser machining of small-diameter medical tubing, with fast turnaround (parts in days, not weeks).

Tone: professional, direct, no hard sell. Keep it under 150 words. End with a single, low-friction call-to-action.
Best Fully specified Outreach
Same scenario, now with role, persona, example style, and a self-critique step.
Prompt
Act as a senior sales rep at Sublime Laser, a 20+ year specialist in precision laser machining of small-diameter medical tubing (nitinol, stainless steel, precious metals, ceramic).

Write a cold outreach email to {{name}}, a {{title}} at {{company}}, a {{company size}} medical device company working in {{therapy area}} (e.g. neurovascular, peripheral vascular, structural heart).

Known facts about the recipient: {{paste 2–3 lines from their LinkedIn or recent press release}}.

Our value props:
- Femtosecond and fiber laser machining for tubes 0.008"–0.500" OD
- 12–30 micron kerf, 12+ micron feature size
- Femto vaporizes material with virtually no HAZ — ideal for nitinol and thin-wall stainless
- Typical turnaround: days, not weeks; on-site "rent-a-laser" iteration available

Tone: peer-to-peer engineer speak. No marketing fluff. Reference one specific material or capability that fits {{therapy area}}.

Format:
- Subject line (<= 60 chars)
- Opening line that references {{name}} or {{company}} specifically (not generic)
- 3 short paragraphs max
- Single call-to-action: a 20-minute call, with 2 specific time windows

Length: <= 160 words for the body. After the draft, give yourself a 1–5 score on personalization and clarity, and rewrite once if either is under 4.

Better With context Follow-up

Prompt

- Internal note: {{optional, e.g. "they mentioned a Q2 budget freeze"}}

Voice example we like:
"Hey {{first name}} — wanted to float this back to the top of your inbox in case it got buried. No need to respond if the timing's off; just flag me when it's relevant and I'll re-cut the quote. Either way, I'll assume the project is on hold until I hear otherwise."

Constraints:

- No exclamation points
